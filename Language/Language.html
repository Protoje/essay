The Case for Banning Torture in Papua New Guinea


Torture is an offensive practice that has persisted across the world, leaving scars on humanity's conscience. In the diverse nation of Papua New Guinea (PNG), the debate over whether torture should be banned rages on in various government organization and law firms as well as the majory of citizens. This essay argues that PNG should and without ambiguity put a stop to these kind of practices (torture), stressing on the legal obligations, taking human rights into consideration, and perceiving the potential for fostering trust and stability within the country.

International Legal Obligations

PNG is an individual to several international treaties and conventions that condemn the unlawful practice of torture. Notably, the United Nations Convention Against Torture (CAT) and Other Cruel, Inhuman, or Degrading Treatment or Punishment, to which PNG is a party, and by no doubt condemns torture and has put into places legal bodies to prevent and prohibit it. By banning torture, PNG demonstrates its commitment to upholding international legal norms, fostering goodwill with the global community.

Human Rights and Dignity

Respecting the constitutional dignity of every person is a fundamental principle. Everyone has a right to freedom and life and no one has the right to take it away from them. The act of torture directly contradicts upon this dignity and violates human rights. Amnesty International has documented cases of torture in PNG, raising concerns about the treatment of detainees. Banning torture aligns with PNG's constitutional guarantees of human rights and sends a powerful message that human dignity must be preserved at all costs. Many people have gone to prison for taking part in torture related activities because of the human rights law and the enforces behind it

Fostering Trust in Law Enforcement

Trust in law enforcement agencies is predominant in maintaining social order. Torture crumble away public trust and can lead to distrust of the legal system. The legal bodies can be distrusted in they do not do anything about it. Banning torture reinforces the idea that law enforcement agencies must adhere to high ethical standards and rely on legal procedures rather than causing to obtain information or confessions through painful ways. This trust-building approach contributes to more effective crime prevention and resolution.

Reducing Risk of False Confessions

Research has shown that torture can lead to false confessions. Innocent individuals may confess under duress to end their suffering, leading to wrongful convictions. By banning torture, PNG can reduce the risk of imprisoning innocent citizens, thus ensuring a fair and just legal system. All around PNG women are being tortured based on suspicion, the fear of being tortured can cause a person to reveal false information

Promoting Alternative Investigative Techniques

Proponents of torture often argue that it is a quick means of extracting information. However, banning torture encourages the development and adoption of alternative investigative techniques, such as improved interviewing skills, forensic analysis, and intelligence gathering methods. These techniques can be more effective and do not involve the ethical and moral pitfalls of torture. Right now in PNG the only method of interrogation method that we all know is the torture method to extract information. However, the ban of torture can lead to new more effective ways of interrogation

Enhancing Papua New Guinea's Global Image

Finally, banning torture improves PNG's global image. A nation that respects human rights and upholds the rule of law is more attractive to international investors, tourists, and partners for development. It demonstrates that PNG is committed to joining the ranks of progressive, responsible nations on the world stage. The world perceives PNG as a carnivorous country because of headlines about torturing, if torturing is banned, it can increase the global image of PNG to attract many tourists etc…

Conclusion

In conclusion, banning torture in Papua New Guinea is a moral urgent that aligns with international legal obligations, preserves human rights, brings civilization and fosters trust within the nation. It reduces the risk of false confessions, promotes alternative investigative techniques, and enhances PNG's global reputation. By embracing this change, PNG can take a significant step towards a more just and humane society. We should start by banning the practice of torturing in PNG…
